Act III - Episode 4

Perfection Disguised as Standards

My standards were often becoming a shield.

For a long time... I called it standards. That sounded better. More professional.
Jump to content

Intro

For a long time...

I called it standards.

That sounded better.

More professional.

More disciplined.

More respectable.

Saying...

“I have high standards”

sounds admirable.

Saying...

“I struggle with perfectionism”

sounds vulnerable.

So I hid behind standards.

TO BE FAIR

And to be fair...

standards do matter.

I care deeply about quality.

Execution.

Design.

Stable systems.

Intentional products.

That part was real.

Core realization

But eventually...

I had to admit something uncomfortable.

My standards were often becoming a shield.

A shield from judgment.

A shield from public imperfection.

A shield from releasing something...

before it felt complete.

IT SHOWED UP EVERYWHERE

That pattern showed up everywhere.

Websites.

Products.

Music.

Infrastructure.

Brand systems.

Apps.

Client work.

Creative releases.

Training systems.

THE LOOP

I kept telling myself:

One more improvement.

One more feature.

One more design pass.

One more optimization.

One more cleanup round.

One more rebuild.

One more layer of polish.

Core realization

And sometimes...

that extra effort mattered.

But often?

It delayed feedback.

And delayed feedback...

delays growth.

THE COST

That became expensive.

Because the market does not reward hidden perfection.

It rewards visible value.

People cannot buy...

what you never release.

Users cannot improve...

what they never touch.

Audiences cannot support...

what they never see.

USEFUL ENOUGH

That realization hit me hard.

Because I could point to multiple projects...

that should have gone live sooner.

Not because they were perfect.

Because they were useful enough.

Core realization

And usefulness matters more...

than private perfection.

WHY THIS FELT SCARY

That was difficult for me.

Because imperfect launches feel vulnerable.

People can criticize unfinished edges.

People can misunderstand your vision.

People can compare your version one...

to someone else’s version twenty.

That can feel brutal.

Core realization

But perfection creates a different pain.

Silence.

Nobody criticizing you...

because nobody knows the thing exists.

That pain lasts longer.

THE QUESTION

Eventually I started asking:

Is this truly broken?

Or am I emotionally uncomfortable releasing it?

Core realization

That question saved me countless hours.

Sometimes something genuinely needed more work.

And sometimes...

I just needed courage.

WHAT CHANGED

And once I started releasing more imperfect things...

something surprising happened.

The world did not end.

People responded.

People gave feedback.

People found value.

And progress accelerated.

Core realization

Launch faster.

Improve publicly.

Let reality shape iteration.

Save perfection...

for things that truly require it.

Closing

That mindset became essential.

Because there was still one painful reality left to confront.

A graveyard.

Of nearly finished work.

And that graveyard had lessons of its own.
